### Post 1: r/smallbusiness

**Title: We went from 12 Google reviews to 80+ in 4 months. Here's exactly what worked.**

Alright, I work with a heap of local businesses (cafes, salons, a couple of dentists) and the #1 thing they all struggle with is Google reviews. They do great work but their Google profile looks dead. Here's what actually changed things:

**1. Ask at the peak moment.** Not a week later. Not via email. Right when the customer is happiest — walking out after a great haircut, picking up their coffee, finishing their appointment. That's when they'll actually do it.

**2. Remove every friction point.** Nobody is going to search for your business on Google, find the review button, and type something out. You need to hand them a direct link. Google has a "get more reviews" link in your Business Profile — use it. Put it in a text, a QR code on the counter, whatever.

**3. Make it a staff habit, not a campaign.** The businesses getting consistent results don't do "review pushes." They just bake it into the checkout process. Every customer gets asked. It becomes muscle memory for the team.

**4. Text beats email by a mile.** Open rates on SMS are like 95%+ vs 20% for email. We started texting customers a review link right after their visit and the response rate was night and day.

**5. Actually respond to every review.** Google's algorithm seems to favour businesses that engage. Plus it shows future customers you give a damn.

The texting thing worked so well that I actually ended up building a tool around it — [InsightReviews](https://insightreviews.com.au). It sends the SMS automatically and routes people to Google/Yelp. But honestly, even doing this manually with a spreadsheet and your personal phone will get you results. The key is consistency.

1. **Texting the link** instead of just asking verbally. People say "yeah sure" and then forget. A text sitting in their phone is way harder to ignore.
2. **Making it routine.** Not a "review campaign" that lasts two weeks. Just something the receptionist does after every single appointment now.

If you're sitting on a mediocre Google rating and you know your customers are happy, this is probably the easiest win going.

## QUORA ANSWERS

### Answer 1: "How can a small business get more Google reviews?"

I've worked with a heap of local businesses on exactly this, so I'll share what I've seen actually work rather than theory.

**1. Ask at the right moment.**
Timing is everything. The best time to ask is right after a positive interaction — when a customer is checking out, picking up their order, or finishing their appointment. Their goodwill is at its peak. Wait a day and they've moved on.

**2. Use SMS, not email.**
This was the single biggest thing that changed results for the businesses I work with. Text messages have 90%+ open rates. Emails sit at maybe 20%. After a visit, send the customer a text with a direct link to your Google review page. Keep the message short and friendly: "Thanks for coming in! If you have 30 seconds, we'd really appreciate a Google review: [link]."

**3. Get a direct Google review link.**
Go to your Google Business Profile, and in the "Get more reviews" section, copy the short link Google gives you. This takes the customer straight to the review form — no searching, no clicking around. Every extra step you make them do, you lose half of them.

**4. Make it a daily habit for staff.**
The businesses that consistently get reviews aren't running "campaigns." They've built it into their checkout routine. The person at the register asks for a phone number and fires off the text. It takes 10 seconds and it compounds over time.

**5. Respond to every single review.**
Google's algorithm seems to favour businesses that actively engage with reviews, and it shows other people that you actually read and care about what customers say. Even a short "Thanks so much, glad you enjoyed it!" makes a difference.

**6. Don't incentivise reviews.**
This violates Google's terms and can get your reviews stripped. Don't offer discounts or freebies in exchange. Just make the process easy and ask genuinely.

**7. Consider automating the process.**
If you want to take the manual work out of it, there are tools that handle the SMS sending and link routing. I built one called [InsightReviews](https://insightreviews.com.au) that does exactly this — texts the customer after their visit and directs them to Google. But even doing it manually from your phone works if you're consistent.

### Answer 2: "How do I deal with negative reviews on Google?"

First, breathe. One bad review is not going to tank your business. Here's how to handle it practically:

**Respond publicly, calmly, and quickly.** Acknowledge the issue, apologise if appropriate, and offer to make it right offline. Something like: "I'm sorry you had that experience. I'd love to chat about this — could you email me at [your email]?" This shows future customers that you care, which honestly matters more than the review itself.

**Don't get into arguments.** Ever. Even if the review is unfair or exaggerated. Other customers are reading your response, not the reviewer's. Take the high road every time.

**Report reviews that violate Google's policies.** If a review is fake, from a competitor, or has nothing to do with your business, flag it through Google Business Profile. It doesn't always work, but it's worth trying.

**Bury bad reviews with good ones.** This is the real long-term play. If you have 8 reviews and one is bad, that's 12.5% of your profile. If you have 80 reviews and one is bad, nobody notices. The best defence against negative reviews is a steady stream of positive ones.

**Prevention is underrated.** A lot of negative Google reviews could have been resolved privately if the business had caught the complaint first. If you give customers an easy way to share feedback directly with you before they go to Google, you can fix the problem and often turn them into a fan.

That's actually the core idea behind a tool I built called [InsightReviews](https://insightreviews.com.au) — it collects feedback via SMS first, and routes unhappy customers to the business privately instead of to Google. Not a silver bullet, but it catches problems before they become public.

Focus on the stuff you can control: respond well, collect more positive reviews, and create a channel for unhappy customers to reach you directly.
