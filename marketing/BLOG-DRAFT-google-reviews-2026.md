# How to Get More Google Reviews in 2026: The Complete Guide for Local Businesses

Look, if you run a local business in Australia — whether it's a cafe in Surry Hills, a dental clinic in Parramatta, or a mechanic in Geelong — you already know that Google reviews matter. But here's the thing most people don't realise: they matter way more than you think.

A well-known Harvard Business School study found that a half-star improvement on a business's rating can increase revenue by 5 to 9 percent. Half a star. That's the difference between 4.0 and 4.5 — a gap that might come down to just a handful of reviews.

And yet, most local businesses are sitting on maybe 15 or 20 reviews, half of which are from 2022. Meanwhile, their competitor down the road has 200+ and a shiny 4.7 rating. Guess who Google shows first?

If you've been meaning to "do something about reviews" for the last six months but haven't gotten around to it, this guide is for you. No fluff, no gimmicks — just practical stuff you can start doing this week.

## Why Most Businesses Struggle with Reviews

Here's the uncomfortable truth about online reviews: there's a massive asymmetry at play.

When someone has a terrible experience — cold food, rude service, a botched haircut — they're fired up. They'll go home, open Google, and write three paragraphs about how they'll never come back. That review practically writes itself because the emotion is doing the heavy lifting.

But when someone has a great experience? They leave happy, go about their day, and never think about it again. It's not that they wouldn't leave a review. They just... don't. There's no emotional trigger pushing them to do it.

Research from Northwestern University backs this up — the average business only hears from about 1 in 10 satisfied customers unless they're actively asked. That means for every glowing review you've got, there are probably nine other happy customers who walked out the door without saying a word online.

The result? Your Google profile ends up skewed negative. Not because your business is bad, but because your unhappy customers are more motivated to speak up than your happy ones.

The fix isn't complicated. You just need a system that closes the gap — one that makes it dead simple for your satisfied customers to leave a review when the experience is still fresh.

## 10 Practical Ways to Get More Google Reviews

### 1. Ask at the Point of Sale (Seriously, Just Ask)

This is the single most effective thing you can do, and it costs nothing.

When a customer is standing in front of you, they've just had a positive interaction, and they're in a good mood — that's your window. A simple "Hey, if you've got 30 seconds, we'd really appreciate a Google review" works surprisingly well.

BrightLocal's 2025 consumer survey found that 72% of customers who were asked to leave a review went on to do so. Seventy-two percent. The problem was never that people don't want to help — it's that nobody asks them.

Train your staff to read the room. If a customer is clearly happy — they're complimenting the food, thanking the physio, telling the hairdresser they love it — that's the moment to ask. Keep it casual, keep it genuine, and don't make it weird.

### 2. Use SMS, Not Email

If you're sending review requests by email, you're leaving a lot on the table. The open rate for marketing emails hovers around 20%. For SMS? It's 98%, with most texts read within three minutes.

Think about your own behaviour. When you get a text, you read it. When you get an email from a business, there's a solid chance it goes straight to your promotions tab and you never see it again.

A short, friendly text message sent within an hour of the visit — "Thanks for coming in today, Sarah! We'd love to hear how it went: [link]" — gets dramatically better results than an email sent the next morning. The experience is still fresh, the customer is still warm, and they can tap the link and leave a review in under a minute.

### 3. Get Your Direct Google Review Link

This is a small thing that makes a huge difference. If you send someone to your Google Business Profile and expect them to find the "Write a review" button themselves, you've already lost half of them.

Instead, use the direct review link that opens the review popup straight away. Here's how to get it:

1. Go to your Google Business Profile
2. Click "Ask for reviews" (or search "Google Place ID Finder")
3. Copy the direct link — it'll look something like: `https://search.google.com/local/writereview?placeid=YOUR_PLACE_ID`

Put this link everywhere. In your text messages, on your receipts, in your email signatures, on your website. Remove every possible point of friction between "I'll leave a review" and actually doing it.

### 4. Make It a Staff Routine, Not an Afterthought

The businesses that consistently get reviews aren't doing anything fancy — they've just built it into their daily routine.

Make review collection part of the checkout process. The same way your staff asks "Would you like a receipt?" or "Do you want to book your next appointment?", they should be asking about reviews. It takes five seconds.

Some businesses gamify it — a small monthly bonus for the staff member who generates the most reviews, or a team lunch when you hit a milestone. Whatever works for your crew. The point is that it needs to be someone's job, or it becomes nobody's job.

### 5. Respond to Every Single Review

This one's counterintuitive, but responding to reviews actually generates more reviews.

When potential customers see that you respond to feedback — especially negative feedback — it signals that you actually care. It makes people more likely to leave their own review because they know it won't just disappear into the void.

Google has also confirmed that responding to reviews improves your local search ranking. It's one of their local SEO signals. So you're getting a double benefit: more reviews and better visibility.

For positive reviews, keep it short and genuine. Thank them, mention something specific if you can, and invite them back.

For negative reviews, take a breath before you respond. Acknowledge the issue, apologise where appropriate, and offer to make it right offline. Never get defensive, never argue, and never make excuses. Every response is a public performance — other customers are reading it to judge how you handle problems.

### 6. Set Up a QR Code at Your Counter

QR codes had their renaissance during COVID and they're not going anywhere. A well-placed QR code that links directly to your Google review page is one of the lowest-effort, highest-return things you can set up.

Print it on a small stand for your counter, put it on the back of your business cards, stick it on your takeaway bags, or add it to the bottom of your receipts. Pair it with a simple call to action: "Enjoyed your visit? Scan to leave us a review."

You can generate a QR code for free using any number of online tools. Just make sure it points to your direct Google review link (see point 3), not your general business profile.

### 7. Timing Is Everything

The best time to ask for a review is when the positive experience is still fresh. For a restaurant, that's right after the meal. For a tradie, it's the moment the job is done and the customer is impressed. For a dentist, it's... well, maybe wait until the numbness wears off.

The worst time? Two weeks later, when the customer has moved on and can barely remember the details. If you're going to send a follow-up message, do it within an hour or two. Same day at the latest.

According to ReviewTrackers, 53% of customers expect a business to respond to a negative review within a week — but the expectation for the review request itself is even shorter. Strike while the iron's hot.

### 8. Don't Incentivise Reviews (It'll Backfire)

This comes up constantly, and the answer is clear: do not offer discounts, freebies, or any other incentive in exchange for reviews. Not even indirectly.

Google's terms of service explicitly prohibit incentivised reviews, and they're getting better at detecting them. If they catch you, they can strip all your reviews — not just the incentivised ones, all of them. Years of social proof gone overnight.

Beyond the platform risk, incentivised reviews tend to be obvious. "Great coffee! 5 stars!" repeated 30 times in a month doesn't look organic to anyone. Customers can smell it, and it erodes trust.

You can absolutely remind people and make it easy for them. You just can't pay them for it.

### 9. Leverage Your Happy Customers to Counter the Unhappy Ones

Here's a smart move that most businesses don't think about: when you get a negative review, the best response isn't just replying to it — it's activating your happy customers.

You don't need to do anything dodgy here. Just increase your review request efforts for a week or two after a bad review comes in. If you've got a solid base of satisfied customers, a handful of fresh positive reviews will push that negative one down the page and bring your average back up quickly.

This is where having a system in place really pays off. If you're manually texting people or relying on memory, you won't be consistent enough to recover quickly. But if you've got a streamlined process — even something as simple as a nightly batch of texts to that day's customers — you'll have a steady stream of positive reviews that dilutes any negatives naturally.

### 10. Consider Automating the Whole Process

If you're doing more than a handful of transactions a day, manually asking every customer and sending individual texts is going to fall apart eventually. Someone gets busy, someone forgets, and suddenly you haven't sent a review request in three weeks.

This is where review management tools earn their keep. Platforms like InsightReviews let you punch in a customer's phone number at checkout, automatically send a branded SMS, and smart-route the response — positive reviews get directed to Google (or Yelp, Facebook, wherever you want), while negative feedback stays private so you can follow up before it goes public. It turns a manual, inconsistent process into something that runs in the background every day.

Whether you use a tool or build your own workflow, the principle is the same: remove the human bottleneck. The businesses that get 200+ reviews aren't working harder than you — they've just automated the boring bits.

## What NOT to Do

A quick rundown of the most common mistakes that can waste your time or get you in trouble:

**Don't buy fake reviews.** Google's AI detection is genuinely good now, and it's improving every quarter. Fake reviews get flagged and removed, and repeat offenders get their profiles suspended. It's not worth the risk.

**Don't review-gate.** This is where you ask customers how their experience was, and only send the happy ones to Google. Google considers this a violation of their guidelines. You should make it easy for all customers to leave a review — but you can absolutely choose where to direct people based on their sentiment, as long as you're not preventing anyone from reviewing publicly.

**Don't ask for "5-star reviews."** Ask for honest feedback. Aside from being against Google's policies, asking specifically for 5 stars makes customers uncomfortable and makes your reviews look suspicious when they're all perfect scores.

**Don't spam people.** One review request per visit is plenty. If someone doesn't respond, leave it. Sending three follow-ups makes you look desperate and annoying. Nobody wants to be nagged into leaving a review.

**Don't ignore your Google Business Profile.** Make sure your hours are correct, your photos are current, your services are listed, and your contact info is right. A neglected profile makes people less likely to leave a review because it looks like nobody's home.

## How Long Does It Take to See Results?

Real talk: this isn't a magic wand situation. If you're starting from a handful of reviews, here's a rough timeline of what to expect.

**Week 1-2:** You'll start seeing new reviews trickle in as you implement your system. Maybe 3 to 5 if you're asking consistently.

**Month 1:** If you're sending review requests to most of your customers, you could realistically pick up 15 to 30 new reviews, depending on your volume. Your star rating might shift by a tenth or two.

**Month 2-3:** Momentum builds. You've got a routine, your staff are in the habit, and your review count is climbing steadily. This is where the local SEO benefits start kicking in — Google notices the velocity of new reviews and gives you a bump in local pack rankings.

**Month 6:** If you've been consistent, you could easily have 100+ new reviews. Your rating should have stabilised at something that reflects your actual service quality, and you'll likely be outranking competitors who aren't actively collecting reviews.

The key word is *consistent*. A burst of 20 reviews followed by three months of nothing looks unnatural to Google and doesn't help your ranking long-term. Steady, organic growth — a few reviews a week — is what moves the needle.

## Wrapping Up

Getting more Google reviews isn't rocket science. It comes down to three things: ask consistently, make it easy, and do it while the experience is fresh. Everything else — the tools, the QR codes, the response templates — is just there to help you do those three things more reliably.

The businesses that dominate local search in 2026 aren't the ones with the biggest ad budgets. They're the ones with 400 genuine reviews and a 4.6 rating, because every day, someone on their team is making sure happy customers get a nudge to share their experience.

Start small. Pick two or three things from this list and implement them this week. You don't need to overhaul everything at once — you just need to start being intentional about it. A month from now, you'll wish you'd started today.
